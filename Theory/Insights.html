1. Independent research: Planning towards Data Visualizations and the thoughts behind each visualization technique:

1. Hacking Attack : Phishing Scams. As a world wide issue that is inclusive of so many people who have fallen victims of it, I wanted to use a visualization technique that would really get the seriousness of it in a visually compelling way, one that enhances understanding and greater retention value. As such, I decided to use an interactive bar graph chart because through it, each individual bar could be used to show different aspects of the subject matter in a way that enhances underdtanding. For example, by using a tooltip that shows the number of victims affected or preyed upon by different phishing URL Host names, users get an understanding of the different variations of the subject matter at hand, actively learning about it as they move from one bar to the next. See figure below:

With that said, I envisioned the use of each bar to convey a piece of information that is meaningful apart from users passively observing or deriving meaning from the size of each bar and that becoming their only point of reference from which they deduce understanding. The use of each individual bar that each displays a tooltip with a unique set of information as well an interactive legend , one that users can manipulate to gain an even deeper understanding of the subject matter at hand increased the level of engagement that users have within the site as well the overall experience of traversing the site. As such, using a bar graph chart in the context of my thematic content was a specific design and functionality choice because it aided me in deriving the narrative I wanted to get across in minimalistic but effective way.

2. Hacking Attack : Ransomware Attacks. For this aspect of hacking , I wanted to focus more on making users underdtand the second part of aspect of the cybersecurity at hand, extensively focusing on the repercussions of it. As a dark aspect of the internet that most users are not deeply immersed into because of the stigma attached to it, beginning to uncover the real egents that steer the ship of victimizing users online was one of the key things i wanted to get across. That way, if users are aware of who these agents are and what methods they use to prey upon them , they can alter their own online activity to be more mindful of even the most "smallest" things like the domain names or protocol of the sites they interact with , something that most people do not necessarily pay attention to, hence its easier to prey on ignorant online users. By using a pie chart with buttons functionality, I wanted users to derive meaning in a different way as opposed to the standard setup and layout of pie charts. Allowing users click a button that corresponds with a specific ransomwaregroup on the chart and thus change or manipulate the state of the chart itself allowed for an extra room to derive understanding apart from using the size of the charts and percentages to infer understanding. See figure below:

Each button click and thus an "expose" (pronounced expo(zay)) of each ransomware group, also symbolized by the protruding of a slice of the pie was meant to highlight or reinforce the idea of what it means to bring to "light" what remains in the "dark" side of the internet (pun intended). By protruding each slice of the pie each time a corresponding ransomwaregroup button is clicked, I aimed to make users understand what making something "stand out" can mean in the context of the subject matter at hand.That is , the transition of each slice "popping out" is a direct reflection of exposing or uncovering the "masks" behind the attacks of different victims of the world. That way users become aware of this knowledge that was delivered in a way that fosters engagement and interactivity apart from the pie sizes and the percentages on them. The use of the tooltip for each pie slice was meant to add up to what is already there in increasing understanding and greater retention value.
